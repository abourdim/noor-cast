1 Getting Started

1.1 What is NoorCast?

NoorCast is a free, browser-based, multi-camera screen recorder specifically designed for teachers creating tutorial videos with robots and micro:bits. It combines screen capture, webcam feeds, microphone input, and micro:bit sensor data into a single recording — all from your browser, without installing anything.

Unlike traditional screen recorders, NoorCast was built for education. It understands that a teacher explaining robotics needs to show their screen (code), their robot (camera), their face (webcam), and their micro:bit sensor readings — all at once, switching between layouts seamlessly.

Key Facts

  • 100% local — nothing leaves your computer. No cloud, no accounts, no tracking.
  • Zero install — just open a URL in Chrome or Edge. Works offline after first load.
  • Zero dependencies — no npm, no build step. One HTML + one JS + one CSS file.
  • 200+ features — from basic recording to AI co-hosts and instant replay.
  • Trilingual — French (default), English, Arabic (with full RTL support).
  • PWA installable — add to your desktop/home screen like a native app.

1.4 Simple vs Pro Mode

🎓 Simple Mode

Designed for beginners and young students:

  • Right sidebar (scenes) auto-collapsed
  • More Tools overflow menu hidden
  • Pause/Marker buttons hidden
  • Advanced settings sections hidden

🚀 Pro Mode

Full access to every feature:

  • Right sidebar with scenes visible
  • All toolbar buttons accessible
  • Full source style popup
  • All settings sections visible

Switch anytime: Settings → mode switch button at the top.

The mode is persisted in localStorage. NoorCast remembers your choice across sessions.

3 Camera Setup — Detailed Guide

NoorCast supports multiple simultaneous cameras. This chapter covers every way to add a camera, from built-in webcams to using your phone.

3.1 Built-in Laptop Webcam

In the left sidebar, find the Camera dropdown.
Your built-in webcam appears as "FaceTime HD Camera" (Mac), "Integrated Camera" (Windows), or similar.
Select it from the dropdown.
Click the green 🎥 + Add button.
Grant camera permission when the browser asks.
Your face appears on the canvas. By default, it's placed as a small circle in the bottom-right (PIP position).
If the camera feed is flipped (text appears mirrored), right-click the source → Mirror to fix it.

3.2 USB External Webcams

Plug in a USB webcam. It appears in the Camera dropdown automatically (may take 2-3 seconds).

Using Multiple USB Cameras

NoorCast supports unlimited simultaneous cameras. Add them one at a time:

  1. Select Camera 1 from dropdown → click Add
  2. Select Camera 2 from dropdown → click Add
  3. Repeat for Camera 3, 4, etc.

Each camera becomes a separate source on the canvas that you can position, resize, and style independently.

3.3 Phone as Camera (Wireless Webcam)

Use your iPhone or Android phone as a wireless camera. Several options:

Option A: iPhone Continuity Camera (Mac only)

If you have a Mac running macOS Ventura (13) or later and an iPhone running iOS 16+:

Make sure both devices are signed into the same Apple ID and on the same Wi-Fi network.
Bluetooth must be enabled on both devices.
In NoorCast, open the Camera dropdown. Your iPhone appears as a camera option.
Select it and click Add. Your iPhone's camera feed appears on the canvas.
Place your iPhone near the robot for a dedicated robot cam. Use your laptop's built-in webcam for your face.

Option B: DroidCam (Windows/Mac + Android/iPhone)

Install DroidCam on your phone (free on App Store / Play Store).
Install the DroidCam client on your computer (Windows or Mac).
Connect your phone and computer to the same Wi-Fi network.
Open DroidCam on your phone — it shows an IP address and port.
Open DroidCam client on your computer and enter the IP/port. Click Start.
In NoorCast, the Camera dropdown now shows "DroidCam" as an option. Select and Add.

Option C: EpocCam (iPhone + Windows/Mac)

Install EpocCam on your iPhone.
Install the EpocCam driver on your computer.
Both devices must be on the same Wi-Fi or connected via USB.
Open EpocCam on your iPhone.
In NoorCast, "EpocCam" appears in the Camera dropdown. Select and Add.

Option D: OBS Virtual Camera

If you already use OBS Studio, you can:

In OBS, set up your phone camera feed (via NDI, Spout, or capture card).
Click "Start Virtual Camera" in OBS.
In NoorCast, "OBS Virtual Camera" appears in the dropdown. Select and Add.

3.4 Camera Angles for Tutorials

For a robotics tutorial, you typically need 2-3 camera angles:

📷 Face Camera

Position: Eye level, looking at you.

Purpose: Shows your face while explaining. Builds connection with students.

Scene: Appears as a small circle (PIP) in most scenes. Full screen in "Solo" scene.

Tips: Good lighting on your face. Neutral background. Look at the camera, not the screen.

🤖 Robot Camera

Position: Overhead or angled view of the robot/breadboard.

Purpose: Shows the physical robot, wiring, or components.

Scene: Full screen in "Mecha" scene. Right half in "Code+Mecha".

Tips: Use a phone on a tripod or clamp. Good lighting on the workspace. Avoid shadows from your hands.

🎛 Sensor Camera

Position: Pointing at a display, oscilloscope, or sensor readout.

Purpose: Shows real-time sensor data or test equipment.

Scene: Full screen in "Sensors" scene.

Tips: Use a second USB webcam or phone. Frame the display tightly.

🖥 Screen (not a camera)

Position: N/A — captures your computer screen.

Purpose: Shows code, IDE, browser, or presentation.

Scene: Full screen in "Code" scene. Left half in "Code+Mecha".

Tips: Increase font size in your IDE. Close notification popups. Use a clean desktop.

9.2 How Instant Replay Works

Decodes audio from your recording using OfflineAudioContext
Computes RMS energy in 1-second windows across entire duration
Finds the top 7 loudest moments (non-overlapping, 4s clips)
Sorts clips chronologically
Re-encodes via canvas + MediaRecorder with flash transitions + clip counter badge
Downloads ~28s highlight reel as .webm + confetti burst

9.3 How Quality Score Works

Analyzes 5 dimensions:

🎤 Audio (clarity)Average RMS level — higher = clearer voice
📈 Pacing (variation)RMS variance — varied = engaging, flat = monotone
🎭 Scenes (variety)Number of chapter markers = more scene switching
⏱ Duration (sweet spot)2-5 minutes scores highest. Too short/long penalized.
🔥 Engagement (peaks)% of windows above 2× average energy

Each dimension: A+ to D grade with colored progress bar. Overall = average. Score ≥80 triggers confetti.

17 Audio

17.1 Audio Pipeline

Every sound NoorCast produces or captures travels this graph:

Microphone → MicBoost (gain + noise gate) → Analyser → Recording stream
Screen capture audio → VolumeDuck → Recording stream
Background music   → Recording stream + Speakers
Piano / Sound Pad / SFX → Speakers + Recording stream

All paths converge at Engine.audioDest (a MediaStreamAudioDestinationNode) which feeds the MediaRecorder. The analyser taps the raw mic signal pre-gain so the VU meter shows what you're actually saying, not the boosted output.

Why care? If something is silent in the recording but audible live (or vice versa), the problem is at one of these nodes — usually the gate level or the duck threshold.

17.2 Mic Boost & Gate

Where. Settings → Audio → "🔊 Boost mic" + "🚪 Noise gate".

Gain (0–4×). Multiplies the mic signal. Default 1×. Use 2–3× for a quiet headset; never go to 4× unless you're whispering — clipping (red on the LED meter) ruins audio.

Noise gate (–60 to –20 dB). Mutes the mic when the signal is quieter than the threshold. Default –40 dB. Raise to –30 dB if you have constant background hum (PC fan, AC). Lower to –50 dB to capture quiet voices.

Why both? Gain makes you louder. Gate hides the noise floor between sentences (when nobody's talking but the mic is still on). Together they sound much more "studio" than raw mic input.

Persisted as tc-mic-gain and tc-mic-gate-db.

17.3 Volume Ducking

What. When you speak, screen audio (game sounds, music in the captured tab, etc.) automatically lowers by 12 dB so your voice is the focus. Releases when you go silent.

Where. Settings → Audio → "🔻 Auto-duck screen audio".

Detection. Triggers when mic RMS > 0.05 (after gain + gate). Hold time 800ms before releasing — prevents flicker between words.

Why. Tutorial videos with background gameplay or music can drown out commentary. Ducking is the broadcast trick that keeps the talk on top without manual mixing.

Defaults. Off · 12 dB attenuation · 800ms release.

17.4 LED Audio Meter

What. 10-segment vertical bar next to the REC button showing live mic level.

🟢 Green (segments 1–6)
Healthy — the mic is hearing you, signal is in the safe zone.
🟡 Yellow (segments 7–8)
Hot — you're loud, but still OK. Aim for peaks here on the loudest words.
🔴 Red (segments 9–10 = clipping)
Distorting — the digital signal exceeds 0 dBFS. Lower gain or move further from the mic. Audio recorded while red looks crackly even if it sounded fine to you live.

Why. Pre-flight check before you press REC. Watch the meter while you say a normal sentence — if peaks stay green, raise gain; if they hit red, lower gain.

What. Speed up the entire recording by capturing fewer frames per second. A 1-hour build becomes a 6-minute timelapse at 10×.

Where. Settings → Enregistrement → Capture → ⏩ Timelapse toggle + multiplier (×2 to ×20).

How it works. Canvas captureStream is called at 30 / multiplier fps. The recorder still produces 30fps playback, but each captured frame represents multiplier seconds of real time. So a 1-minute soldering session at ×10 plays back as 6 seconds.

Use cases.

  • Building a project — assemble a robot, hand-write a long proof, soldering, painting → ×10 to ×20.
  • Multi-step demos — show every step of a long process in a few seconds → ×4 to ×8.
  • "How long did this take?" — visualize lengthy work in a quick clip → ×6.

Caveat. Audio recording continues at full speed but plays back fast (chipmunk effect). Best to mute mic during timelapse and add a voiceover later, OR keep mic on for fun chipmunk narration.

Defaults. Off · ×4 multiplier · stage 1920×1080 unchanged.

24.2 Time Goal & Auto-Stop

What. Set a target recording duration. A countdown chip appears on the canvas. Optionally have NoorCast stop automatically when the goal is reached.

Where. Settings → Enregistrement → Capture → ⏳ Objectif de durée toggle + minutes input + auto-stop checkbox.

How. Set minutes (0.5–60, in 0.5 increments). Start a recording. A pill in the top-right shows time remaining. When zero is hit: auto-stop fires (if enabled), or just keep going past zero (chip flashes red).

Why.

  • Lessons with strict length. "5-minute lightning lesson" — set 5min + auto-stop. Your kids learn to fit content into the slot.
  • Practice pacing. "Explain this in under 2 minutes" → builds discipline.
  • Hard time-box. Class ends at 14:30 — set the timer, walk away.

Defaults. Off · 5 minutes · auto-stop off (you decide whether to keep recording past the goal).

24.3 Screensaver — idle wake screen

What. After 90 seconds of no user input (and not recording), the canvas is replaced by a drifting emoji and a "press any key to wake up" hint. Any mouse / key / touch event dismisses it.

Where. Settings → Comportement → 📺 Screensaver après 90s d'inactivité checkbox.

Why. Two reasons:

  • Live demos: Burn-in protection for OLED displays during prep / breaks / Q&A.
  • "Is this thing on?" When the canvas is dark, kids look at it puzzled. The drifting emoji says "I'm alive, click me" without distracting from the workspace.

Defaults. Off · 90s timeout · drifts a random emoji from a 20-emoji palette.

24.4 Intro / Outro Cards

What. Cinematic full-canvas cards that play at the start and end of every recording. The intro card (2.5s) shows the title + active scene name + "Action!" countdown. The outro card (2s) shows "Merci!" + a badge summary of the take.

Where. Settings → Comportement → 🎬 Cartes intro/outro toggle.

How. Toggle on → recordings auto-prepend the intro card and append the outro card to the file. The cards use your active brand colors / theme. Both bake into the file (no post-processing).

Why. Polishing your tutorials in a single click. Every recording feels intentional instead of "I started talking mid-sentence". The outro reminds viewers what they accomplished + provides a natural ending instead of an awkward cut.

Defaults. Off · intro 2.5s · outro 2s · uses brand title + slogan + theme accent.

24.5 Onboarding Tour — first-launch spotlight

What. A 5-step guided tour that runs the first time you ever open NoorCast. Spotlights the most important UI areas with a tooltip explaining each: Sources, Canvas, Scenes, Recording, Settings.

Where. Auto-fires on first launch (gated by tc-tour-done in localStorage). Re-run from Help panel → "Replay tour".

How. Each step dims the rest of the UI, highlights one element with a glowing outline, and shows a tooltip with a brief explanation + Next/Skip buttons.

Why. 200+ features make NoorCast intimidating. The tour gets a complete beginner from "what's this?" to "I can record" in 30 seconds without reading documentation.

Defaults. On · runs once · skippable at any step.

24.6 Session Export & Import

What. Two complementary backup tools. Both produce a single JSON file you can save / share / restore. Both are non-destructive (Import can preview before applying).

📦 Export Session
Settings → Maintenance. Downloads noorcast-session-YYYYMMDD-HHMM.json containing every tc-* localStorage key — settings, badges, history, brand logo (base64), watermark text, scene customizations, theme, all preferences. Around 50–500 KB depending on what you've stored.
📥 Import Session
Upload a previously exported JSON. Confirms with a "replace all settings?" dialog. On accept, it overwrites localStorage and reloads the page. Your current setup is wiped — keep an Export of your current state first if you might want to revert.

Use cases. Backup before resetting cache · transfer your setup to a new computer · clone the same configuration across multiple classroom workstations · share your "perfect setup" with a colleague · roll back after experimenting.

What's NOT included. Recordings, snapshots, badge cards (these are downloaded files, not localStorage). Live camera/mic streams (those are device-bound). The micro:bit firmware (lives on the device).

Defaults. Always available · no auto-export · no version conflict checking (importing an old session over a newer one may break some features — re-importing the newer one fixes it).

24.7 Multi-Select Sources

What. Select 2 or more sources at once and operate on them as a group — move them together, delete them in one shot, change their visibility together.

How to enter / exit multi-select.

  • Shift+click any source → adds to / removes from the selection.
  • Ctrl/Cmd+A → selects all visible sources.
  • Click empty canvas or press Esc → clears the multi-select.

Visual feedback. Multi-selected sources show a sky-blue dashed outline (different from the single-select green outline).

Group operations.

  • Move — drag one source; all selected sources move by the same delta. Preserves their relative positions.
  • Delete — Delete or Backspace removes all selected sources.
  • Hide / show — toggles visibility of all selected together.

Why. Re-arranging a 4-camera layout one source at a time is tedious. Selecting all and dragging the group as one is a single gesture.

24.9 Scene Auto-Save

What. Whenever you drag, resize, change opacity, swap a skin, or modify any property of a source, the currently active custom scene is silently re-saved. No "Save Layout" button needed.

Where. Settings → Comportement → 💾 Auto-save scene on drag/resize toggle (default ON).

How. A small debounced trigger fires 800ms after the last edit. The active custom scene's layout is overwritten in tc-scene-custom. Built-in scenes (you, code, robot…) are not overwritten — they stay pristine.

Why. Kids would forget to save and lose 10 minutes of layout tweaking. Auto-save eliminates that frustration.

Caveat. If you don't want the scene mutated, switch to a built-in scene first, then experiment. Or use the Layout Undo (28.40) to revert.

Defaults. On · 800ms debounce · only active custom scenes get auto-saved.

24.10 Scene Auto-Advance

What. Automatically rotates through your scenes during a recording. After N seconds on a scene, NoorCast switches to the next one in order.

Where. Settings → Comportement → ↻ Avance auto des scènes toggle + interval (5-600 seconds).

How. Toggle on → set interval (e.g. 30s) → start recording. Each scene gets exactly N seconds, then the next one activates. A countdown pill in the top-right shows time remaining.

Use cases.

  • Time-boxed lessons — "30s intro, 30s code, 30s robot, 30s wrap-up". Total 2 minutes, every section the same length, you don't need to watch the clock.
  • Photo-booth style content — set 5-10s intervals for a fast-moving sample reel.
  • Live performance — DJ-style scene rotation without touching keyboard.

Defaults. Off · 30 seconds · loops back to scene 1 after the last.

26.5 Offline Usage

After the first load, NoorCast works 100% offline. The PWA architecture caches everything you need on first visit, then runs from cache forever after.

What's cached on first visit:

  • index.html, noorcast.js, style.css, sw.js, manifest.json
  • All SVG icons (logo, workshop-diy logo, language flags FR/EN/AR)
  • All 12 self-hosted WOFF2 fonts (Amiri, Tajawal, Bangers, Orbitron, Righteous — both LTR and RTL variants)
  • Total: ~2.5 MB. One-time download.

Install as a desktop / mobile app:

  1. Open NoorCast in Chrome / Edge.
  2. Click the install icon in the address bar (looks like a screen with a down arrow), OR browser menu → "Install NoorCast".
  3. The app opens in its own window without browser chrome — like a native app. Pin it to your taskbar / dock.
  4. Subsequent launches load from cache instantly. Zero network calls.

What still needs internet:

  • Live Captions — Chrome's SpeechRecognition sends audio to Google. Use Edge on Windows 10/11 for offline speech recognition.
  • Voice Commands / Smart Scene Switcher — same SpeechRecognition limitation.
  • That's it. Recording, micro:bit BLE, all overlays, all features work offline.

Updates. When a new NoorCast version is deployed (CACHE_NAME bumped in sw.js), the next visit detects the update and shows a "🔄 New version available — reload" toast. Refresh to get the new version. Your settings + recordings are preserved.

Privacy bonus. Offline mode means zero analytics, zero CDN fingerprinting, zero "calls home". Perfect for privacy-sensitive classrooms.

28.26 Mic check — 3-second auto-test

28.29 Chroma key (green-screen)

What. Make any solid-colored region of a video source transparent, so what's behind shows through. Classic green-screen / blue-screen effect.

Where. Right-click any camera/screen source on the canvas → 🎨 Chroma menu (also exposed in the source context menu). Currently set programmatically too: Engine.sources[i].chromaKey = { color: '#00ff00', threshold: 60 }

How.

  1. Have your camera framed against a solid background (green sheet, white wall, blue cloth).
  2. Apply chroma — the matching color goes transparent, leaving the subject visible.
  3. Tweak threshold (10–120) — higher = more pixels keyed, but spill into the subject.
  4. Drop another source (another camera, a screen, a background image) behind the keyed source for the composited look.

Why. Same trick used in TV weather forecasts and YouTube tutorials. Lets a kid stand against any background and "appear" inside their lesson screen, on a virtual stage, or composited with a robot demo. Combined with CanvasBg image you can fake any backdrop.

Tips. Even, bright lighting on the green surface — folds and shadows confuse the keyer. Avoid wearing the key color (don't wear green if keying green). A real green-screen sheet beats a wall.

Defaults. Off · color #00ff00 · threshold 60 · processed every frame on a hidden offscreen canvas (small CPU cost per keyed source).

Why. Restarting a recording loses the take's flow. Soft rewind lets you keep going and clean up later.

29 Reels mode — recording for vertical platforms (v0.9.x)

Most NoorCast users record in 16:9 landscape for YouTube and laptop screens. But Facebook Reels, Instagram Reels, TikTok and YouTube Shorts are all 9:16 vertical — and uploading a landscape clip there gets you either huge black bars (≈30% of the screen wasted) or auto-cropping that chops your sources. The v0.9.x release line solves this end-to-end.

29.1 What "Reels mode" gives you

9:16 portrait canvas
Settings → 📐 Stage format → 9:16 — 1080×1920. The canvas, the preview and the recording all flip vertical. Your sources reflow automatically — face-cam stays in the bottom-right corner of the new portrait area, robot/screen sources fill the rest.
📱 Reels safe-zone overlay
New toolbar button next to 📊 Sensors and 🎛 Gauges. Toggles a semi-transparent red mask over the three areas where the platform UI covers your video: top header (~13%), bottom caption + buttons (~31%), right action column (~14%). The green dashed box in the middle is the actual safe area — keep important content inside it. The overlay is editor-only and never bakes into the recording.
Auto-MP4 in 9:16
FB and IG transcode WebM with visible quality loss. When the canvas is 9:16, NoorCast auto-prefers H.264 / MP4 even on Windows. The 0-byte Windows-encoder watchdog (v0.7.200) still falls back to WebM if the encoder fails.
Captions stay in the safe zone
Live captions used to render flush against the bottom of the canvas — exactly where the FB caption + like / comment / share column covers them. In portrait, the caption box now floats up to ~34% from the bottom so it stays readable after upload.
Live 90 s cap warning
FB Reels / IG Reels / TikTok / YT Shorts all cap at 90 s — clips longer than that get auto-trimmed at upload, often mid-sentence. In 9:16 mode, the elapsed-time chip turns bright red and pulses past 90 s, with a toast at exactly 90 s and another every 30 s after.
📱 Cut to 90 s for Reels (in-browser trim)
If your take ends up over 90 s in 9:16 mode, a "Cut to 90 s for Reels (−Xs)" button appears in the take panel after the recording stops. One click re-encodes the first 90 s at 8 Mbps using the same canvas + MediaRecorder pipeline that powers Silence-Trim and Instant-Replay. No ffmpeg.wasm bundle, no external dependency. The output is named <original>-reels.mp4 (or .webm).
ffmpeg trim helper
For users who want a stream-copy (zero re-encode) trim, a clickable toast after every 9:16 save copies an ffmpeg -ss 00:00:00 -to 00:01:30 -i <file> -c copy <file-reels> one-liner to the clipboard.
Skip-intro audio gate
Settings → 🎥 Capture → 🎙 Skip silent intro (start on first word). After the countdown finishes, NoorCast opens an AnalyserNode on your active mic source and waits up to 5 seconds for the first audible word (RMS > 0.05) before starting MediaRecorder. Lets you say "let me reach for the mouse… ok GO" and not record the dead air. Falls back to immediate-start when no mic is wired or the timeout fires.

29.2 Workflow — first Reels recording, end to end

  1. Open Settings → 📐 Stage format and pick 9:16 — 1080×1920. The canvas flips vertical and a one-time toast says "📱 Reels mode — safe-zone guide ON". The 📱 Reels button in the toolbar lights up.
  2. Look at the red zones. Anything in the red areas will be hidden by FB / IG / TikTok UI when you upload. Drag your watermark, sensors overlay and servo gauges into the green dashed box.
  3. (Optional) Settings → 🎥 Capture → 🎙 Skip silent intro. If you tend to start with dead air, turn this on so MediaRecorder waits for your first word.
  4. Hit REC. Watch the elapsed timer. If it turns red, you're past 90 s and need to trim before uploading.
  5. STOP. The take panel opens. If your take is over 90 s, a pink 📱 Cut to 90 s for Reels (−Xs) button appears. Click it; a re-encoded -reels.mp4 file downloads in ≈the duration of the original (real-time encode). Otherwise, click the post-save toast to copy the ffmpeg command for a stream-copy trim.
  6. Upload to Facebook Reels. Pick the -reels.mp4 file. No black bars, no auto-crop, captions visible above the FB UI, all under the 90 s cap.

29.3 Tips for great Reels content

  • First 3 seconds matter. The platform algorithm decides whether to keep showing your Reel based on early retention. Skip the intro splash; turn on Skip-intro; start with action.
  • Sweet spot is 15–30 s. 90 s is the cap, not the target. Shorter Reels get more shares.
  • Watch in muted preview. ~70% of Reels viewers scroll with sound off. The burned-in captions (LiveCaptions module) are essential — make sure they're in the safe zone.
  • Vertical-aware composition. A face-cam in the corner of a 16:9 frame becomes the corner of a tall portrait frame — still works. But a wide horizontal robot demo shrinks to a strip in the middle. Consider the 👋 You scene preset (face-cam fullscreen) or a custom layout that stacks face top + content bottom.
  • Bitrate. The default High — 8 Mbps in Settings → Recording → Quality is right for Reels. Don't go higher; FB will downscale anyway and big files just slow upload.

29.4 Known limits

  • The in-browser trim is a real-time re-encode (90 s of video takes ≈90 s to trim). For instant trims, use the ffmpeg helper one-liner.
  • The trim button only handles the first 90 s. To pick a different start point, use the ffmpeg command and edit the -ss timestamp.
  • The safe-zone overlay's exact percentages are based on FB Reels chrome as of 2026. Other platforms (TikTok, IG) are similar but not identical — the green box is a conservative safe area that works on all four.
